\chapter{USE CASES, CASE STUDIES, AND COST SCENARIOS}

The value of a multi-cloud orchestration platform becomes clearer when it is evaluated in concrete operating scenarios. This chapter presents representative use cases that show how Nebula can support different organizational contexts. The objective is not to claim universal optimality, but to demonstrate how a unified control plane helps teams reason about infrastructure decisions, deployment models, and cost trade-offs.

\section{Use Case 1: Startup SaaS Platform}
Consider a startup building a customer-facing SaaS application with a small engineering team. The team wants to launch quickly, keep costs visible, and avoid becoming dependent on a single provider too early. In this context, Nebula can simplify experimentation by exposing a unified workflow for creating development virtual machines, object storage buckets, and regional test environments. Instead of learning three portals at once, the team can standardize its provisioning requests through the control plane and compare outcomes side by side.

For this type of organization, the main benefit is cognitive efficiency. The team is usually small, and the same engineers often handle both product features and infrastructure work. Nebula reduces context switching, shortens onboarding time for new team members, and preserves a record of what was provisioned and why.

\section{Use Case 2: Enterprise Department With Microsoft Alignment}
Now consider an enterprise department that already uses Microsoft identity and collaboration tooling heavily but wants selected analytics or external-facing workloads to run elsewhere. Azure may remain the primary provider because of organizational alignment, yet AWS or GCP may offer better fits for specialized workloads. Nebula is useful here because it allows the department to keep Azure-centered governance while still exposing controlled access to other providers for approved cases.

In this environment, the orchestration platform acts as a policy boundary as much as a convenience tool. Provider onboarding, tag enforcement, region restrictions, and audit visibility become just as important as raw provisioning speed. A unified dashboard helps because the department can see where resources live without forcing every operator to become an expert in each provider's management console.

\section{Use Case 3: Research Laboratory or Academic Institution}
Academic environments often have bursty demand patterns. A research lab may need temporary compute clusters, storage for large datasets, and reproducible infrastructure for student projects or experiments. Budget visibility and repeatability are especially important because team members change frequently and infrastructure knowledge can become fragmented across batches of students.

Nebula supports this environment well because its workflows can be documented, reused, and observed centrally. The control plane allows a lab supervisor or technical lead to standardize setup steps, provide curated infrastructure templates, and review what has been created across providers. This reduces the chance that students accumulate unmanaged or forgotten resources over time.

\section{Use Case 4: Seasonal E-Commerce Expansion}
An e-commerce platform with seasonal demand may want to maintain a primary cloud footprint while keeping a secondary provider available for contingency or regional performance reasons. During normal traffic periods, only part of the multi-cloud estate may be active. During seasonal spikes, the organization may provision overflow resources in a second cloud.

The operational challenge here is coordination. The team must know which resources are primary, which are standby, how inventory stays synchronized, and how failures are diagnosed quickly under pressure. Nebula's asynchronous task tracking and centralized inventory view directly support that need.

\section{Case Study: Failed Provisioning With Guided Recovery}
One of the most practical situations for Nebula is a deployment failure during a time-sensitive release. Suppose a team attempts to create infrastructure in AWS but receives a permissions-related error from Terraform. In a conventional workflow, an engineer may need to parse provider logs, identify the relevant missing permission, open a separate IAM console, and then verify whether the failure is due to credentials, policy scope, or region restrictions.

In Nebula, the same event follows a more structured path. The task is recorded with a clear state transition, the execution log is stored centrally, the AI copilot summarizes the likely root cause, and the operator can review the suggested fix. Even when the copilot is not perfectly correct, it still reduces the search space. This is valuable because incident pressure often comes from time lost in ambiguity rather than from the technical difficulty of the final fix.

\section{Case Study: Inventory Drift Detection}
Another recurring cloud problem is inventory drift. A resource may be deleted manually in a provider console or altered outside the expected automation path. When that happens, local records and real infrastructure diverge. Nebula's periodic synchronization process reduces the duration of this inconsistency by reconciling actual provider state with the platform database.

For a team using multiple clouds, this feature becomes disproportionately valuable. Manual verification across three portals is tedious and error-prone. Centralized reconciliation gives the team a more trustworthy picture of its estate and helps ensure that dashboards, reports, and cost assumptions remain closer to reality.

\section{Organizational Benefits Beyond Provisioning}
The long-term value of a system like Nebula is not limited to provisioning speed. It also creates benefits in:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{training}: new engineers learn one operational workflow first,
    \item \textbf{documentation}: a single report or manual can describe cross-cloud actions consistently,
    \item \textbf{governance}: audit trails and approval patterns become easier to define,
    \item \textbf{support}: failures are easier to triage through centralized task records,
    \item \textbf{planning}: teams can compare provider choices from a shared interface.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations of Scenario-Based Evaluation}
These case studies are intentionally representative, not exhaustive. Real organizations may have more complex security boundaries, networking requirements, data laws, or procurement constraints than an academic prototype can simulate fully. Nevertheless, scenario-based analysis remains useful because it translates the abstract architecture of the earlier chapters into recognizable operational outcomes.

\section{Discussion}
The case studies reinforce the central thesis of the report: multi-cloud operations are as much about coordination and cognitive load as they are about raw API connectivity. Nebula adds value by reducing fragmentation. It gives teams one place to describe intent, view status, analyze failures, and compare provider behavior. For startups, that means faster learning. For enterprises, it means stronger governance. For academic and research environments, it means reproducibility and visibility. These scenario outcomes strengthen the practical argument for the system and help explain why the architecture described in this blackbook is relevant beyond a classroom exercise.
