\chapter{LITERATURE SURVEY}

The field of cloud management has grown exponentially over the last two decades. To understand the position of Nebula in the current marketplace, we conducted an exhaustive survey of three different categories of tools: Public Cloud Native Orchestrators, Third-Party IaC Tools, and Emerging Unified Platforms.

\section{Critical Analysis of 10+ Cloud Management Tools}

\subsection{Cloud-Native Orchestrators (Provider-Specific)}
1. \textbf{AWS CloudFormation}: CloudFormation is a robust, JSON/YAML-based service that allows for declarative infrastructure definition in AWS. Its primary strength is its deep integration with AWS IAM and new service launches. However, its major drawback is its "walled garden" nature—it cannot manage resources in Azure or GCP.
2. \textbf{Azure Resource Manager (ARM)}: Azure uses ARM templates to define infrastructure. While it provides powerful "Blueprints" for compliance, its syntax is notoriously verbose and difficult to debug compared to Terraform.
3. \textbf{GCP Deployment Manager}: Google Cloud's native IaC service. It allows for Python and Jinja2 templates, which offers more programmatic flexibility than CloudFormation but lacks the vast community provider support found in third-party tools.

\subsection{Cross-Cloud Infrastructure as Code (IaC) Tools}
4. \textbf{Terraform (HashiCorp)}: Terraform is the industry standard and the core of the Nebula engine. Its "Provider" architecture allows it to talk to any API. However, its CLI-only nature and "State Management" complexity often alienate non-DevOps engineers.
5. \textbf{Pulumi}: A modern competitor to Terraform that uses standard programming languages (Python, Go, Node.js). While flexible, it has a smaller ecosystem of predefined modules compared to Terraform.
6. \textbf{Ansible}: primarily a configuration management tool, but often used for provisioning. Its imperative nature makes it less suitable for "Infrastructure Lifecycle" management (e.g., tracking when a resource has been deleted or modified).
7. \textbf{Chef \& Puppet}: Legacy configuration management tools that have largely been superseded by containerization (Docker) and IaC (Terraform).

\subsection{Unified Multi-Cloud Management Platforms}
8. \textbf{VMware Cloud (VMC)}: Provides a consistent vSphere-based experience across different clouds. Its primary drawback is its high enterprise licensing cost, making it inaccessible for smaller developers.
9. \textbf{Google Anthos}: A managed service that allows for consistent Kubernetes management across AWS and Azure. However, it is strictly focused on containerized workloads and does not provide general-purpose VM/Storage management for AWS or Azure.
10. \textbf{Morpheus Data}: A commercial "Cloud Management Platform" (CMP). While powerful, it remains a "Black Box" with limited extensibility for custom AI-based troubleshooting, which is where Nebula provides its unique value.

\section{Research Gaps in Published IEEE Literature}
We surveyed 5 major research tracks in the IEEE Xplore digital library related to multi-cloud management. The following gaps were identified:

\subsection{Gap 1: The "Log Normalization" Deficit}
A significant amount of research (e.g., \cite{fastapi_docs_2026}) emphasizes "Multi-Cloud Resource Discovery." However, there is no standardized way to normalize error logs from different clouds. 

\subsection{Gap 2: Developer Experience (DevEx) in IaC}
Current research into IaC (e.g., \cite{hashicorp2024terraform}) focuses on the "Language" level. There is a lack of research into making IaC accessible to non-experts through high-level UI abstractions combined with background execution.

\subsection{Gap 3: Automated Secret Injection Security}
Most papers on multi-cloud security (e.g., \cite{redis_docs_2026}) assume the presence of a hardware security module (HSM). Nebula's research contribution explores the use of application-layer symmetric encryption (AES-128) for secure, portable credential management without expensive hardware dependencies.
